# Question Evolution

Questions are hypothesized to be living entities rather than strings. They are born, change, compete for attention, and may leave descendants. Over time they evolve as understanding shifts.

## Operations

### Split

One question divides into two or more narrower questions when partial answers reveal distinct sub-problems.

**Example:** "Why don't penguins fly?" splits into:

- Why did penguins lose the ability to fly?
- How do penguins survive without flight?

### Merge

Two or more related questions combine into a single broader or more abstract question.

**Example:** "Why don't penguins fly?" and "Why don't ostriches fly?" merge into:

- Why do some birds not fly?

### Spawn Child Questions

A parent question gives rise to descendant questions during investigation. The parent may remain open while children pursue specific lines of inquiry.

**Example:** From "Why don't penguins fly?":

- What adaptations do penguins have for swimming instead?
- When did flightlessness evolve in penguins?

### Become Dormant

A question is set aside without resolution. It retains identity and may reactivate when related anomalies recur.

**Example:** "Why don't penguins fly?" goes dormant after partial answers about wing structure, until a new flightless bird observation raises curiosity debt again.

### Die

A question is closed — resolved, abandoned, or absorbed into the world model. It no longer competes for attention.

**Example:** "Why don't penguins fly?" dies when the world model cleanly explains flightlessness through adaptation to aquatic life and energy tradeoffs.
